# LP-0016: Decentralized Social Protocol with Generalized RLN-Based Reputation

**`Logos Circle: N/A`**

## Overview

This prize funds a decentralized social protocol where identity is pseudonymous, reputation is earned and ZK-provable, and content moderation is enforced by staked community participants — with no central authority at any layer. The core innovation is the use of Generalized Rate Limiting Nullifiers (GRLN) as a unified primitive for spam prevention and reputation: users who stay within their rate limit build reputation over time, while violators have their identity cryptographically exposed and their stake slashed. Reputation, expressed as an RLN rate limit tier, grows from behavior rather than from a privileged registry.

The output is a protocol — not a finished social application. It defines identity registration, pseudonymous content publication with proof-of-rate-compliance, ZK-provable reputation credentials, and stake-weighted community moderation. Any team can build a microblogging client, a forum, or a comment layer on top of it. The prize requires at least one such reference application to be delivered alongside the core protocol.

## Motivation

Existing decentralized social platforms face a trilemma: anonymous participation invites spam; identity-linked participation enables censorship and surveillance; and centralised moderation reintroduces the power asymmetry the ecosystem is trying to escape. No existing protocol resolves all three simultaneously.

Rate Limiting Nullifiers (RLN) are a ZK primitive designed to enforce per-epoch posting limits on anonymous participants. A user who exceeds their limit in a single epoch produces enough on-chain shares to allow any observer to reconstruct their secret and slash their stake. This enforces rate limits without requiring identity disclosure or a trusted moderator. Generalised RLN (GRLN) extends the construction to parameterisable limits: the secret is committed as the constant term of a degree-N polynomial, allowing N messages per epoch before the secret is recoverable. This turns the rate limit N into a first-class protocol parameter — and a natural reputation signal.

The design space for a complete social protocol on this foundation is wide: epoch definitions in a P2P context, reputation upgrade paths that preserve unlinkability, moderation jury mechanisms, stake incentive design, and client-side UX for proof generation. Multiple valid approaches exist at each layer. Competition is the right mechanism to surface the best combination.

## Token and Program Assumptions

The following assumptions underpin the protocol design. Submissions must document where they adhere to or deviate from these assumptions, and justify any deviation.

- **Identity stake**: Registering an identity requires locking a minimum amount of LEZ tokens as a security deposit. The deposit is not a usage fee — posting is free of token cost. The deposit is slashable if the identity is caught violating the rate limit.
- **Jury bond**: Identities that opt into the moderation jury pool must lock additional LEZ tokens as a bond. Jurors are rewarded for participating in finalized verdicts and partially slashed if they are frequently in the minority, as a Schelling-point incentive toward honest voting.
- **No pay-per-post and no content monetization in protocol**: The protocol does not define any token transfer tied to reading or producing content. Applications built on top may add monetization; the base protocol must not require it.
- **Reputation is not directly purchasable**: A higher LEZ balance does not automatically yield a higher reputation tier. Staking more tokens may increase the security deposit above the minimum but does not advance the reputation tier. Tier advancement is earned through elapsed time and violation-free behavior.

## Protocol Architecture

### Identity Layer

Each user holds a private identity secret `k`. From `k` they derive:

- An **identity commitment** `C = Poseidon(k, nonce)` registered on-chain in the Identity Registry LEZ program.
- A **nullifier key** used to derive per-epoch pseudonymous nullifiers for consistent off-chain identity across sessions without linking.

Registration records `(C, stake_amount, registration_epoch, tier)` on-chain. The identity secret `k` is never revealed. The commitment alone is public.

**Identity upgrades** — advancing from a lower to a higher reputation tier — require the user to retire the current commitment and register a new one. A ZK proof must demonstrate that: (a) the retiring identity has a valid commitment currently in the registry, (b) the retiring identity has reached the minimum age and has no recorded slashing events, and (c) the new commitment is fresh (not previously registered). The proof is submitted alongside the new registration, and the old commitment is marked as retired. The on-chain record does not link old and new commitments; only the holder of `k` knows they are the same person.

### Posting Layer (Generalized RLN)

A Generalized RLN commitment encodes a rate limit N into the identity's registration. The identity's secret `k` is committed as the constant term of a degree-N polynomial over a finite field:

```
f(x) = k + a₁·x + a₂·x² + … + aₙ·xᴺ
```

The coefficients `(a₁, …, aₙ)` are derived deterministically from `k` and the current epoch via a PRF, so the polynomial changes each epoch. For each message `m` published in epoch `e`, the user computes a share:

```
x = H(m || external_nullifier)
y = f(x)
```

and attaches `(x, y, epoch, rln_proof)` to the message. The `rln_proof` is a ZK proof that: (a) the prover holds a valid registered commitment at the current tier, and (b) `(x, y)` is a correctly evaluated share of the corresponding polynomial for this epoch — without revealing `k` or the commitment index.

Any observer who collects N+1 valid shares `{(x_i, y_i)}` for the same commitment and epoch can interpolate the polynomial and recover `k`. They then derive `C = Poseidon(k, nonce)` and submit a **slash proof** to the Identity Registry, identifying the violating commitment. The slashed commitment is blacklisted; part of the stake is transferred to the slasher as a reward, and the remainder is burned.

### Epoch Definition

Epochs must be defined in a way that is consistent across P2P participants without a trusted clock. Submissions must choose and justify one of the following approaches or propose an alternative:

- **LEZ-block-range epochs**: An epoch is a contiguous range of LEZ block numbers (e.g., 1 000 blocks ≈ 10 minutes). This is objective and requires only a connection to the LEZ network.
- **Commit-reveal epochs**: Epoch boundaries are determined by a commit-reveal beacon embedded in the LEZ protocol.
- **Hybrid**: Content is produced with block-range epoch tags; disputes resolve on-chain using the LEZ block range.

The choice must be documented with an analysis of its failure modes.

### Reputation Tiers

Reputation is expressed as a GRLN tier. Three tiers are required; submissions may define additional ones.

| Tier | Rate Limit | Minimum Age (epochs) | Jury Eligible |
|------|-----------|----------------------|---------------|
| 1    | 1 post / epoch | 0 (entry) | No |
| 2    | 5 posts / epoch | 30 violation-free epochs | No |
| 3    | 20 posts / epoch | 90 violation-free epochs | Yes |

Tier advancement requires a ZK proof of age and violation-free status submitted alongside the new commitment registration. The proof must not reveal the epoch count precisely — only that the threshold has been met — to prevent timing-based identity correlation. Tier regression is not possible; a slashed identity is removed from the registry entirely.

### Moderation Layer

Content moderation is enforced by a jury of Tier 3 identities who have opted into the jury pool by locking a jury bond.

**Flagging**: Any registered identity can flag a content address (the hash identifying a piece of content stored on Logos Storage or similar). Flags are published as signed messages over a shared Logos Messaging topic. Flagging costs no tokens but requires a valid RLN proof (counts against the flagger's posting rate limit), discouraging trivial flag spam.

**Jury convening**: When a content address accumulates a threshold of flags within a rolling window, a jury is convened. Jury selection uses a verifiable random function seeded by the content address hash and the current epoch, selecting a fixed number (e.g., 7) of identities from the jury pool. Submissions must specify and justify the selection mechanism.

**Voting**: Each selected juror submits a binary vote (remove / retain) as a ZK proof, demonstrating they are a selected juror for this case without revealing their commitment index. Votes are published over Logos Messaging. A majority among submitted votes within a time window determines the verdict.

**Verdict execution**: Verdicts are submitted on-chain to the Identity Registry:

- **Remove verdict**: The content address is added to a public blocklist LEZ program. The author's identity (if identifiable from on-chain records) may be subject to stake reduction at the juror's discretion — but the author's commitment is not automatically slashed (only RLN violations trigger automatic slashing).
- **Retain verdict (abusive flagging)**: If the majority vote is retain and the flag count was above a threshold, the flaggers lose a fraction of their accumulated reputation credit (reducing their effective epoch count toward the next tier upgrade).

Jurors who vote with the majority receive a share of the jury reward pool. The exact incentive structure is a design choice left to the submitter.

### Known Open Problems

Submissions are expected to acknowledge and address the following. Reasonable trade-offs are acceptable; the write-up must document the choice and its consequences.

**Unlinkability across tiers**: When a user upgrades their commitment from Tier 1 to Tier 2, the ZK proof of age must not enable a passive observer to infer timing or correlation between the old and new commitment. Naively, both commitments appear in the registry at the same epoch, and the retirement timing is linkable. Submissions must describe their mitigation strategy (e.g., time-randomised retirement, blind upgrade proofs).

**Sybil resistance at registration**: A minimum stake deters cheap Sybil creation, but does not eliminate it for well-funded actors. Submissions must characterise the residual Sybil surface and document any additional countermeasures.

**Jury collusion**: A jury pool of Tier 3 identities is a small, pseudonymous set. Collusion among a majority can produce biased verdicts. Submissions must document the collusion model and propose mitigations (e.g., appeal mechanisms, jury pool rotation, over-selection with random subset).

**P2P content availability**: RLN proofs are attached to content messages, but content itself must be stored somewhere. The protocol does not mandate a storage layer; submissions must specify and integrate at least one (e.g., Logos Storage, IPFS). The write-up must address availability without a trusted host.

## Illustrative Use Cases

These are examples of what a reference application or third-party client could build on top of the protocol. The prize does not require all of them; the core protocol must be sufficient to support them.

- **Pseudonymous microblogging**: A user publishes short messages with RLN proofs. Clients verify proofs before rendering content. A user who posts above their rate limit is slashed on first detection by any client.
- **Forum with reputation-gated posting**: A forum topic requires Tier 2 identity to post and Tier 3 identity to moderate. New users can read but not write. Reputation upgrades are automatic once the on-chain criteria are met.
- **Token-gated community channel**: Combined with LP-0005 (private token balance attestation), a Logos Messaging group requires both a valid GRLN identity (no spam) and a token balance above a threshold (skin in the game).
- **Cross-application reputation portability**: A user earns Tier 3 reputation on a forum application and then presents a ZK proof of tier to gain elevated posting rights on an unrelated microblogging client — without revealing any other information about their identity or posting history.
- **Transparent blocklist**: The community moderation blocklist is a public LEZ program. Any client can query it. No single client or developer controls it; verdicts are produced by jurors and recorded on-chain.

## Success Criteria

- [ ] A user can register a Tier 1 identity with a minimum LEZ stake, publish a post with a valid RLN proof, and have the proof verified by a third-party client without any on-chain transaction by the verifier.
- [ ] A user who publishes two posts in the same epoch (violating Tier 1 limits) has their secret recoverable from the two shares. A slash transaction can be submitted by any observer and is accepted by the Identity Registry.
- [ ] A user can advance from Tier 1 to Tier 2 via a ZK upgrade proof, with the upgrade recorded on-chain, without the on-chain record revealing a link between the old and new commitment.
- [ ] The GRLN circuit correctly enforces rate limits for at least Tier 1 (N=1) and Tier 2 (N=5). Proofs for both tiers are generated client-side in under 30 seconds on a standard laptop.
- [ ] The community moderation system is deployed: content flagging, jury convening, anonymous jury voting, and verdict execution are all implemented and demonstrated end-to-end on LEZ testnet.
- [ ] ZK proofs of reputation tier (e.g., "I am Tier 2 or above") can be generated off-chain and verified both on-chain (via a LEZ verifier program) and off-chain (locally), compatible with the LP-0005 proof transmission model.
- [ ] At least one reference social application (microblogging client, forum, or equivalent) is delivered on top of the protocol, demonstrating identity registration, posting with RLN proofs, tier upgrade, and content moderation in a coherent user flow.
- [ ] At least 10 distinct identities are registered on LEZ testnet by parties outside the submitting team, at least 3 of which complete a Tier 1 → Tier 2 upgrade, and at least 1 jury moderation event is completed involving jurors from outside the submitting team.
- [ ] Full documentation and a clean public repository are delivered.

## Scope

### In Scope

- **Identity Registry LEZ program**: on-chain commitment registration, tier tracking, slashing, and retirement.
- **GRLN circuits (Risc0)**: ZK circuits for at least Tier 1 and Tier 2 post proofs, and the ZK upgrade proof for tier advancement.
- **Slash verifier**: logic (on-chain or off-chain library) for reconstructing a secret from two shares and submitting a valid slash proof.
- **Moderation programs**: content blocklist LEZ program; jury pool registration; vote submission and verdict execution.
- **Off-chain verifier library**: verifies RLN post proofs locally without an on-chain transaction.
- **Client-side SDK or CLI**: generates RLN proofs, manages identity keys, submits registrations and upgrades, and publishes content with proofs.
- **At least one reference application** demonstrating the full identity and content lifecycle.
- **Reputation tier ZK credential**, compatible with LP-0005 proof format where possible.
- Documentation covering: protocol design, GRLN polynomial construction and epoch definition, identity upgrade unlinkability analysis, moderation incentive design, security assumptions, known limitations, and integration guide.

### Out of Scope

- A production-quality social media application with full discovery, following, notifications, or algorithmic feeds.
- Content monetization mechanisms at the protocol layer.
- Multi-chain identity or cross-chain slashing.
- Changes to the LEZ token program or commitment format used by LP-0005.
- Ongoing maintenance or security audits beyond initial delivery.
